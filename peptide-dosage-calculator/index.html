How the Calculator Works

When you reconstitute a peptide, you dissolve a measured amount (in mg) of lyophilized powder into a measured volume (in mL) of bacteriostatic water. The resulting liquid has a known concentration, which is the foundation for every other calculation.

Concentration

Concentration is simply the amount of peptide divided by the volume of water:

Concentration (mg/mL) = Vial amount (mg) ÷ Water added (mL)

For example, a 5 mg vial reconstituted with 2 mL of water gives a concentration of 2.5 mg/mL (or 2,500 mcg/mL).

Converting Your Dose

Once you know the concentration, you can convert any dose into a volume:

Volume to draw (mL) = Dose (mg) ÷ Concentration (mg/mL) Syringe units = Volume (mL) × 100    (for a standard U-100 syringe)

The calculator handles all three input units — mcg, mg, and syringe units — and shows all the equivalents at once.

Doses Per Vial

Doses per vial = Vial amount (mg) ÷ Dose per injection (mg)

Understanding Syringe Units

Standard insulin syringes are calibrated for U-100 insulin, where 100 units = 1 mL. The tick marks on the barrel are in these "units." This convention is routinely borrowed for peptide dosing since the same syringes are typically used. If you draw to the 25-unit mark, you are drawing 0.25 mL of solution.

Tips for Accurate Reconstitution

  • Always add bacteriostatic water to the vial slowly, aiming the stream at the glass wall rather than directly onto the powder.
  • Gently roll or swirl the vial — do not shake — until the powder is fully dissolved.
  • 1–2 mL of water is a practical range for most peptide doses when using a 0.5 mL or 1 mL insulin syringe.
  • Store reconstituted peptides refrigerated (2–8 °C) and use within the timeframe specified by the manufacturer.
